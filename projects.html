Academic Problem

During graduate school, my research focused on the recycling of water through the land surface (evapotranspiration, or ET) and how land-surface-regulated water flow impacts weather extremes. Does local or remote ET constitute a major source of precipitation for communities across North America? How do precipitation anomolies or land surface changes in upwind ET source regions alter precipitation patterns downwind?

To study these questions, I used the National Center for Atmospheric Research's Community Earth System Model (CESM), a global climate model enhanced with water tracers to track the movement of water molecules across the continent. At the time, CESM could not track the individual components of ETâ€”evaporation from soil and freshwater sources, evaporation from plant surfaces (canopy evaporation), and plant-regulated water transfer (transpiration). Since each component of ET responds differently to environmental conditions, separating total ET into these three components is essential for understanding its role in weather extremes.

My Solution

I modified the CESM source code to implement water tracers for each component of ET. This required writing new FORTRAN code and developing a framework that allows other researchers to track these components independently in their own simulations.
